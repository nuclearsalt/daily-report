THU-1 · Sugar Addiction Recovery

Reading back through this week's check-ins, the loop is easy to trace — and it's not a willpower problem. It's a dopamine problem wearing a sugar costume.

Last night (July 2) you nailed the mechanism yourself: "Today I let myself have social media while having breakfast and wasted about three hours on Instagram… Drained the dopamine early. Made the rest of the day's tasks a little bit harder. Probably accounts for the sugar craving." That's a genuinely sharp observation. Then the store trip, the whole guava/dulce-de-leche brick, reason logged: Low energy. On June 30 the same shape: "Floodgates for sugar opened up… Was it a reward for actually doing things today?" And June 28: "Every time I do this I scold myself. Maybe that's adding to the response… I'm maybe eating in response to the scolding."

Here's the addiction-science version of what you're already noticing:

The pleasure-pain balance (Dr. Anna Lembke, Dopamine Nation). Every hit of a fast dopamine source — Instagram at breakfast, then sugar — is a "press on the pleasure side" of a see-saw the brain always tries to level. To level it, the brain tips you into a matching dip: the crash, the fog, the "I can't do the tasks" feeling. That low is not a fact about your energy. It's the comedown from the earlier spike. You drained it early, so the tank read empty by afternoon, so sugar looked like the only refill. It wasn't restoring energy — it was chasing the balance the morning scroll knocked over.

The loop is a habit, not a flaw (cue → routine → reward). The cue is a low/foggy feeling. The routine is fast sugar. The reward is 20 minutes of "okay-ness," then another dip. The self-scolding you flagged on June 28 is doing real damage here: shame is itself a painful state, which the brain wants to soothe — with the exact quick fix that caused it. You correctly guessed you're "eating in response to the scolding." You are. Drop the scolding and you remove one of the loop's fuel sources.

Three things you can do today

  • Protect the morning dopamine, exactly as you wrote it. Your own plan for tomorrow — bathroom → light yoga/weights → meds → breakfast with no social media — is the highest-leverage move on this page. The rule isn't "have more discipline," it's "don't spend the day's dopamine before 9am." Put the phone in the other room during breakfast (the Brick you've used before works). If the morning tank stays full, the afternoon sugar cue never fully fires.
  • Insert a 10-minute delay, not a "no." When the craving hits, you don't have to refuse it — refusing is what builds the dam that later bursts (your "what you resist will persist" week said this). Instead: set a timer for 10 minutes and drink water or the tart-cherry/blueberry juice first. Cravings are waves; most crest and fall inside 10–20 minutes. You're not forbidding, you're outlasting.
  • Swap the reward, keep the ritual. When the cue is "I did a hard thing, I deserve something," the ritual of a reward is healthy — it's the payload that's the problem. Pre-decide a non-sugar reward for finishing a task: 15 minutes of drums (you logged 60 min and "drums = fun" yesterday — a real dopamine source that doesn't crash you), a walk in the sun, an episode of something.
Today's nudge You already diagnosed it in writing last night: the Instagram breakfast drained the tank, and the sugar was you trying to refill it. So today, run the experiment you set for yourself — phone away until after breakfast, and when the mid-afternoon fog rolls in, treat it as the comedown it is, not as proof you're incapable. Ten minutes, water, drums. The gout you can feel coming isn't a reason to scold yourself; it's just data telling you the see-saw's been working overtime. Level it gently, not with a fight.

Framing drawn from Dr. Anna Lembke's Dopamine Nation (pleasure-pain balance) and standard CBT habit-loop / urge-surfing practice. Not medical advice — energy, clarity, and less anxiety are the goal, not weight or appearance.

THU-2 · Tips from World-Class Drummers

Five practice ideas from named pros — weighted toward getting gig-ready efficiently, since you're learning songs for the metal audition on Saturday the 11th.

Benny Greb — "The Whale Method"

jazz / fusion Break a hard groove or fill into single beats. Loop the bar but play only beat 1; once it's solid, add beat 2, then both, resting the remainder — building up beat-by-beat while keeping the part in its rhythmic context.
Why it helps: isolates the exact spot tripping you up instead of grinding the whole part, so the audition set locks in with fewer reps. · Source

Benny Greb — Separate "practicing" from "playing," and record yourself

jazz / fusion Practicing is a deliberate loop of execute → evaluate → adjust; playing is for flow and fun. Audio-record at least one practice hour, and only move a section forward once you can nail it 3–5 times in a row (the "feel-good" test).
Why it helps: after a 6-year break, every minute counts — recording exposes timing gaps you can't hear in the moment. · Source

Jojo Mayer — "Practice what you can't play"

drum & bass / hybrid "If you sound good while practicing, you're not getting any better." Spend practice time deliberately at the edge of your ability, not on the stuff you already own.
Why it helps: a 20-year vet can coast on old chops — this forces the discomfort that rebuilds speed for the faster metal parts. · Source

Benny Greb — Use the metronome as a tool, including "gap clicks"

jazz / fusion Set the click to 16ths when you want more reference points for stroke accuracy; use sparse clicks — or a gap click that drops out for whole bars — to test and strengthen your internal clock.
Why it helps: metal auditions live and die on rock-solid time; gap-click practice keeps tempo steady when the click (or a live cue) isn't there. · Source

Nate Smith — Build the pocket first, variations second

modern jazz / pocket Treat practice as developing ideas within a repeating groove context — nail a deep, repeatable pocket, then add variation as a storytelling arc rather than random fills.
Why it helps: bands (metal included) hire the drummer who locks the groove and serves the song — pocket over flash makes you the reliable pick at the audition. · Source
