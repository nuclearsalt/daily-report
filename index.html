TUE-1 · Sugar Addiction Recovery

This week's notes are loud and specific. From the last several days: "I think it's the sugar. The fucking sugar." · "Ate chocolate covered cherries, then took a nap." · "Ate more chocolate covered frozen raspberries. AAAAAAA." · "Does 'forcing myself to do it' even work?" The sweets-reason field has the exact diagnosis logged in the tracker itself: Low energy.

That's the loop, in your own words, sitting in the database: low energy → frozen-fruit-shaped sugar → quick spike → nap → wake up feeling like the day got away → guilt-shop and guilt-eat → reach for the quick fix again. It's not a willpower failure. It's a glucose roller-coaster that your nervous system is correctly trying to escape, and "force yourself" is the wrong tool for the job because forcing requires energy that the crash just took away.

This isn't moral. This is the habit loop Anna Lembke describes in Dopamine Nation: the deeper the post-sugar dopamine dip, the louder the next pull toward sugar. Willpower fights against the dip; structure prevents the dip.

Three things to try today — chosen for this week's pattern

  1. Eat protein and fat before the next sugar moment, not instead of it. Anna Lembke and addiction-medicine clinicians both point at the same lever: you do not negotiate with a craving from a glucose low, you negotiate with one from a stable blood sugar. The cottage cheese + cucumber snack in TUE-2 below is exactly this — 22g of protein, 90 seconds to prep, kept in the fridge so the friction is lower than the chocolate-covered raspberries. The goal isn't to avoid the raspberries today. It's to eat the cottage cheese first, and let the craving rate itself again from a stable baseline.
  2. Name the loop out loud the next time you reach. CBT-style: as you open the freezer, say (or mutter) "this is the low-energy reach, not the hungry reach." Two seconds. Not a lecture, not a debate. Just labeling it shifts the brain out of automatic-mode. You logged the loop yourself yesterday — that means the awareness is already there. You just need to insert the label between the cue and the action.
  3. Stop trying to "force" anything this week. Your own note — "Does 'forcing myself to do it' even work? How would I even do that?" — is the data telling you the answer. Force is expensive and you're already at energy 3-4. Replace "force" with "set up" — set up one thing in advance, then let the setup do the work. Today's setup: cut up a cucumber and a red pepper, put the cottage cheese in a small bowl. That's the whole intervention. The future-you with low energy will pick what's easiest, so make the right thing the easiest thing.
Today's nudge. You don't have a sugar problem, you have a low-energy problem with a sugar-shaped solution. The chocolate-covered raspberries are working — they're solving the energy crash for 20 minutes. They just charge a 4-hour interest rate. Today, before the next reach, eat one protein-and-fat thing first. Not instead. First. Then if you still want the raspberries, have them. You'll be choosing them from baseline instead of from the floor — and that's the only difference that matters.
Protein first. Then decide.

Sources: Anna Lembke, Dopamine Nation (Penguin, 2021); ACT/CBT addiction frameworks (urge surfing, cue labeling). Pattern drawn from your own check-in notes 6/22–6/23.

TUE-2 · Recipes: Interesting Individual Dishes

Low/no added sugar · veggie-heavy · spicy · protein-rich · slow carbs · unsaturated fats · individual portions.

Breakfast — Harissa Shakshuka (single-serving)

Eggs poached in a fiery North African tomato-pepper sauce spiked with harissa, cumin, and smoked paprika, finished with wilted spinach. One skillet, one portion.

Ingredients
  • 1 tbsp olive oil
  • ½ small yellow onion, diced
  • ½ red bell pepper, diced
  • 2 garlic cloves, minced
  • 1 tsp ground cumin · 1 tsp smoked paprika
  • 1 tbsp harissa paste · pinch cayenne
  • 1 cup canned crushed tomatoes
  • 1 big handful baby spinach
  • 2 eggs · salt, pepper
  • Fresh parsley or cilantro · optional crumbled feta

Why it fits: ~12g protein from the eggs, veg-forward, no added sugar, slow-carb (no bread), genuine heat from harissa + cayenne, olive oil only.

A grain-free single bowl: warm spiced lentils over greens with cucumber, tomato, red onion, olives, and feta, dressed with a tangy Greek yogurt-lemon-garlic-dill sauce.

Ingredients

Why it fits: ~25g protein from lentils + yogurt, slow carbs only, no added sugar, heavy veg, Aleppo heat, olive-oil + yogurt fats.

Dinner — Gochujang-Glazed Salmon with Sesame Bok Choy

A salmon fillet broiled with a spicy gochujang-soy-ginger-garlic glaze (rice vinegar in place of honey), served over garlicky sesame bok choy. ~20 minutes start to finish.

Ingredients
  • 1 (5–6 oz) salmon fillet

  • Bok choy: 2–3 baby bok choy halved, 1 tsp avocado oil, 1 garlic clove sliced, ½ tsp ginger, 1 tsp toasted sesame seeds, splash tamari, pinch chili flakes

Why it fits: ~35g protein, omega-3 from salmon, gochujang heat without sugary teriyaki, bok choy is slow-carb veg, no rice/bread.

TUE-3 · App Development: React Native, AI & Indie Building

Tuesday focus: mobile-specific — React Native/Expo patterns, UI/UX, structure, performance. (Backend/AI is Wednesday.)

1. Expo SDK 54 + precompiled iOS XCFrameworks

What it is. SDK 54 (late 2025, current in 2026) ships React Native 0.81 with iOS dependencies precompiled as XCFrameworks rather than source. SDK 56 is in beta.

Why it matters. Clean iOS builds drop from ~120 seconds to ~10 seconds on an M-series Mac. Cause: you were recompiling the same React Native C++/Obj-C every clean build. Effect: it's a binary blob that just links now. As an indie dev doing many expo run:ios cycles a day, those minutes add up to whether you bother iterating.

For Tracker. Upgrade to SDK 54 before adding more native modules. Iterating on Buddy screen or new check-in UI on a real device gets instant, which changes how often you'll bother to try variants.

expo.dev — SDK 54 changelog

2. New Architecture is the default (Fabric + JSI + TurboModules)

What it is. The old JS↔native "bridge" is gone. Fabric (renderer), JSI (direct JS↔native calls), and TurboModules (lazy-loaded native modules) replaced it. In SDK 54 you can't turn it off.

Why it matters. The old bridge serialized everything to JSON and shipped it across threads asynchronously — that's why animations used to stutter and scroll lists felt "off." JSI lets JS hold direct refs to native objects. Typical apps see ~40% faster startup and 2-3× faster layout. About 85% of popular npm packages are now compatible.

For Tracker. When evaluating a chart library for mood trends, or a calendar picker for the history view, check "New Architecture compatible" in the README. Incompatible libs either crash or fall back to interop shims that erase the perf win.

docs.expo.dev — New Architecture guide

3. Reanimated 4: CSS animations vs. worklets

What it is. Reanimated 4 (stable mid-2025) splits animation into two: declarative CSS-style ("fade in over 300ms"), and worklets — JS functions compiled to run on the UI thread for gesture-driven work. Worklets now live in the separate react-native-worklets package.

Why it matters. Animations on the JS thread block when JS is busy parsing a Claude API response. Worklets run on the UI thread, hitting 60fps even when JS is choking. CSS animations are "easy mode" with no worklet boilerplate. Separating what from how keeps simple cases simple.

For Tracker. Use CSS animations for check-in-card entry transitions and the streak counter pop. Reserve worklets for gestures — swipe-to-log-mood, pull-to-refresh — anything where the finger drives the motion live.

Reanimated — migration to 4.x guide

4. Expo Router: route groups + protected routes

What it is. File-based routing — your app/ folder IS the nav tree (like Next.js). (tabs) is a route group that doesn't appear in the URL. _layout.tsx wraps child routes. +modal.tsx presents modally.

Why it matters. React Navigation wires up navigators imperatively in code, which gets tangled for an indie dev juggling features. File-based makes the structure visible at a glance. Auth gating lives in (auth)/_layout.tsx — check Supabase session, redirect if missing. No more "did I wrap this in <ProtectedRoute>?" bugs.

For Tracker. (tabs)/index.tsx = today's check-in, (tabs)/history.tsx = past entries, (tabs)/insights.tsx = Buddy insights. Log-mood sheet at (modals)/log.tsx. Login behind (auth)/_layout.tsx.

docs.expo.dev — Expo Router patterns

5. FlashList for any growing list of past entries

What it is. Shopify's FlashList is a drop-in FlatList replacement that recycles cell instances instead of destroying/recreating them. Same API.

Why it matters. FlatList virtualizes (renders only visible rows) but mounts/unmounts components on scroll — hence the "blank flashes" and high JS thread usage. FlashList keeps a pool of mounted cells and swaps the data inside them. Same trick as iOS's UITableView. JS thread stays under 10% during scroll vs. 90%+ on FlatList. Rule of thumb: under ~400 items FlatList is fine, beyond that switch.

For Tracker. The history view grows forever if the app sticks. Build it on FlashList from day one — you'll never have to rewrite when a power user hits day 800. Set estimatedItemSize (row height in px) so it can pre-allocate.

shopify.github.io/flash-list — docs

Bonus pointer for later: 2026 indie consensus on UI kits is Tamagui (compiler-optimized) or NativeWind (Tailwind classes). Both support New Arch. LogRocket — best RN UI libraries.

TUE-4 · Wellness App Landscape vs. John's Tracker

1. Bearable — iOS & Android

Most comprehensive symptom-and-lifestyle tracker for chronic physical and mental health management.

  • Unlimited custom metrics (mood, symptoms, sleep, energy, meds, diet, activity) in a few taps
  • Correlation reports surface which inputs actually move symptoms
  • Strong medication tracking with reminders, doses, adherence history

Gaps: no AI companion · dense, clinical UI · correlation insights paywalled at $34.99/yr.

bearable.app

2. Daylio — iOS & Android

Fastest-to-log mood + micro-journal app, with the iconic "Year in Pixels."

  • 2-tap mood + activity logging removes all friction
  • Year in Pixels makes a year of mood legible at a glance
  • Clean activity-to-mood correlation stats

Gaps: mood is 5-point only (no anxiety/focus/energy dimensions) · no medication or sobriety modules · no AI.

daylio.net

3. Finch — iOS & Android

Gamified self-care via a virtual pet that grows when you complete wellbeing tasks.

  • Shame-free gamification reframes self-care as caring for someone else
  • Pairs mood check-ins with breathing, journaling, and tiny goals in one flow
  • Built-in self-screening quizzes (anxiety, depression, body image)

Gaps: shallow data layer · no correlations · no meds · no sobriety · can feel infantilizing for clinical-grade users.

finchcare.com

4. Stoic — iOS & Android

AI-powered guided journal with morning/evening reflection rituals.

  • Morning-prep + evening-reflection wizard mirrors a structured check-in cadence
  • AI Mentors give contextual prompts and reactions across custom metrics (late-'25/'26)
  • Broad coverage: journal, mood, meditation, breathing, habits, exports

Gaps: no sobriety/cravings · minimal med tracking · AI behind premium tier · some billing/support complaints.

getstoic.com

5. How We Feel — iOS & Android (free, no ads, no paid tier)

Research-backed emotion-granularity tool from Yale's RULER framework.

  • 144-word Mood Meter on a valence/energy grid teaches emotional precision
  • "Reflect" AI feature turns check-ins into journaling prompts
  • Bundles sleep/exercise tracking and coping-strategy video lessons; entirely free

Gaps: no meds, no sobriety, no 1–10 scales · minimal long-form analytics.

howwefeel.org

6. I Am Sober — iOS & Android

Sobriety-first tracker with daily pledge, milestone counters, and per-substance communities.

  • Real-time sobriety counter (down to the second) plus money/time-saved calculator
  • Daily pledge + end-of-day recap creates a bookended check-in pattern
  • Withdrawal timeline populated by real user data; condition-specific communities

Gaps: single-domain · no mood/anxiety/focus dimensions · no meds · no AI conversation.

iamsober.com

5 concrete feature ideas for John's Tracker

  1. Med-filtered correlation dashboard. Show "your anxiety is 2.1 points higher on nights you slept under 6h" — but scoped to your meds (fluoxetine / ritalin / seroquel) individually. Bearable does correlations in general; nobody does them filtered by personal medication adherence. You have the raw data already.
  2. Multi-layer "Year in Pixels." Daylio's single-mood heatmap is iconic. Stack three rows — mood, cannabis-free streak, med adherence — and the visual storytelling about how sobriety lines up with mood across 365 days becomes uniquely yours.
  3. Morning pledge + evening recap framing for sobriety check-ins. Borrow I Am Sober's bookend: morning asks "pledge cannabis-free today?" Evening recaps whether it held, with a real-time counter on the home screen. Adds ritual to the data you're already collecting.
  4. Emotion-granularity tagger on free-text notes. Overlay a How-We-Feel-style 2D valence/energy grid as an optional tag layer on the notes field. Makes notes searchable later ("show every 'restless' entry") and trains precise self-awareness without forcing it.
  5. Buddy-generated Sunday weekly digest. Use the existing Claude integration to push a 1-paragraph summary every Sunday morning: top mood drivers this week, sobriety status, sleep-vs-focus pattern, one experiment to try. Turns Buddy from reactive chat into a proactive coach — closing the gap Stoic and Bearable each only half-fill.

TUE-5 · Passive Income

Tuesday's angle: things that could be built or augmented using Claude. Quick reality check first — nothing here is "passive" in the recliner sense. Front-loaded effort, then reduced (not zero) maintenance. Anyone selling the recliner version is the product.

1. AI Automation Retainer Practice (B2B — Make.com / n8n + Claude API)

Type: Done-for-you workflow automation for SMBs, monthly retainer model.

Setup: 4–8 weeks to land first client. Medium difficulty for John — his enterprise IT + RN dev background maps directly. Sales is the hard part, not the tech.

Realistic income: $500–$3,000 per workflow build + $200–$500/mo maintenance per client. Five recurring clients = roughly $1,000–$2,500/mo semi-passive after build-out.

The catch: Not actually passive — clients call when integrations break (API deprecations, schema drift, edge cases). Churn is real; SMBs cancel when budgets tighten. Sales cycle is 4–12 weeks. Effectively fractional IT consulting with AI flavor.

Where Claude fits: Sonnet/Haiku as reasoning layer inside Make.com/n8n flows — document extraction, contract review, email triage, lead qualification, support routing. Claude Code can scaffold integration code, cutting build time 50%+.

Best fit for John? Highest of the four. Leverages 12 years enterprise IT + indie dev — he already speaks SMB-IT and can debug auth/webhooks fast.

MindStudio — selling AI automations to local businesses · Yaitec — recurring revenue model for AI agencies

2. Vertical Micro-SaaS (single-niche tool, Claude-powered)

Type: Tiny SaaS solving one painful workflow for a narrow vertical (drum-teacher scheduling, RN app review-monitoring dashboard, etc.).

Setup: 2–4 months to MVP + launch. 6–18 months to meaningful MRR. High difficulty — distribution kills more micro-SaaS than code does.

Realistic income: Sobering numbers — 70% of micro-SaaS earn under $1K MRR. Median profitable one is ~$4.2K MRR. 30% plateau at $1K–$5K. Only ~10% break $20K. Plan for $0–$1K in year one as the base case.

The catch: "Build it and they'll come" is a lie. Acquisition and support eat the "passive" hours. Claude API costs scale with active users; a viral hit can briefly run at negative margin until you optimize prompts/caching.

Where Claude fits: Core product reasoning (summarization, classification, generation) + Claude Code shipping the product faster. Prompt caching + Haiku for cheap paths, Sonnet for hard ones, is the standard margin pattern.

Best fit for John? Strong, given RN shipping experience. Pick a vertical with insider knowledge — drumming community, enterprise IT pains.

3. Niche SEO Content Site with AI-Assisted Research

Type: Affiliate / display-ad content in a narrow expertise niche (drumming gear reviews, etc.).

Setup: 6–18 months before meaningful traffic. Low technical, high patience.

Realistic income: $500–$5,000/mo for sites with real expertise. Established sites cluster around $2,000/mo. Many earn $0 indefinitely.

The catch: Google's 2024–25 algorithm shifts + AI Overviews crushed thin AI content. Only sites with real expertise signals — author bylines, original photography, demonstrated experience — survive. Pure AI-spun content is actively penalized.

Where Claude fits: Outline, fact-check, source-synthesize, edit — not first-draft writing if you want to rank. Claude as research engine + your voice = the pattern that still works.

Best fit for John? Drumming niche is genuinely defensible — 20+ years experience = real E-E-A-T signals Google rewards.

4. Paid Newsletter on Beehiiv (specialized expertise)

Type: Niche newsletter monetized via Beehiiv ad network and/or paid tier.

Setup: 3–9 months to a meaningful list. Low technical, high consistency required (weekly cadence kills most).

Realistic income: ~$500–$1,500/mo from automated sponsor placements at ~5,000 engaged subscribers. Paid-tier conversion typically 1–3% of list.

The catch: Subscriber acquisition is the actual job. Without paid ads or an existing audience, organic growth is brutally slow. "Passive" only after ~3–5K subscribers + a content system. Cadence burnout is the #1 failure mode.

Where Claude fits: Research + first-draft + summarization pipeline (Claude reads 20 source links → structured brief; you add voice/opinion). Cuts a 6-hour issue to ~2. Does not replace the human angle — that's what readers pay for.

Best fit for John? Solid for either "Enterprise IT decoded for non-IT execs" or a drumming/music-tech angle. The skeptic voice is itself a differentiator in an AI-slop newsletter market.

Red flags across the board

  • "AI does it all while you sleep" — every credible source debunks this. Front-loaded effort, reduced (not zero) ongoing work.
  • Anyone selling a course before showing their own product revenue. Ask: "What's your MRR on the thing itself, not on the course about the thing?"
  • "AI content at scale" sites — penalized by Google since 2024.
  • Faceless YouTube / AI-voiceover channels — YouTube is demonetizing low-effort AI through 2025–26.
  • API cost blindness — every Claude-powered build needs a unit-economics model from day one (input/output tokens × users × frequency). Prompt caching + Haiku-vs-Sonnet routing is the difference between margin and bankruptcy.

John-specific take

Highest expected value given your profile: #1 (Automation Retainer) as the income engine, #4 (Newsletter) as the distribution funnel that feeds it. Enterprise IT credibility + NYC market + skeptic voice is a defensible combo. #2 (micro-SaaS) is the long-term wealth bet — treat it as 18-month patient capital, not passive income. #3 only if the drumming niche genuinely excites you. Content sites die from boredom more than from competition.
