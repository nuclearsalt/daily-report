FRI-1 · ADHD: Exercise & Mindful Eating

Friday framing — what carries through a loose, structureless weekend.

Three strategies that hold up without a workday scaffold

  1. Anchor movement to a meal, not a time. ADHD brains lose to the clock; they win with sequences. Tie a 15-minute walk to "after breakfast" or "after dinner" instead of "at 10am." Even 15 minutes of brisk movement bumps dopamine 20–30%, which is the same system stimulants act on — so a post-meal walk is genuinely a med-extender on weekends when the Ritalin window shifts. (Equilibria)
  2. Virtual body double for one task you'd otherwise skip. Open a video call (or join a free coworking room like Flow Club or a friend's Zoom) for 30 minutes — cleaning the kitchen, food prep, the bike ride to the store. Body doubling on video is research-backed to be roughly as effective as in-person, and it's the cheat code for weekend executive function. (ADDitude, Healthline)
  3. Three meals + two snacks, alarmed. "Intuitive eating" mostly fails for ADHD because the interoception signal is muffled by stimulants and hyperfocus. Set actual phone alarms for the weekend — 9a, 12:30p, 3:30p, 6:30p — with protein at each (eggs, Greek yogurt, turkey, salmon, beans). This stops the late-afternoon crash where you've eaten nothing all day and then inhale whatever's nearest. (LiveAsYou Wellness, ADDitude)
Today's nudge. Tonight, before sleep, set four eating-time alarms on your phone for tomorrow. Label one of them "+protein." That's it. You don't have to decide what to eat in advance — you just have to make sure future-you gets the prompt while past-you still has the executive function.
Mantra: "Movement after meals, alarms for the gaps."

FRI-2 · Recipes: Intermediate-Level, Interesting Chefs

Five recipes this week from chefs whose work doesn't show up on the front page of every food blog — Padma Lakshmi, Asma Khan, Yia Vang, Toni Tipton-Martin, Hawa Hassan. Spicy, protein-forward, no white starches.

Breakfast · Toni Tipton-Martin's Salmon Croquettes

Tipton-Martin is the editor-in-chief of Cook's Country and the historian behind Jubilee, which traces Black American cuisine through 200+ years of cookbooks. Her salmon croquettes are a classic Black Sunday-morning dish — canned salmon, eggs, scallions, hot sauce, pan-fried in a thin layer of olive oil instead of bacon grease. Serve over wilted greens instead of grits.

Ingredients
  • 1 (14.75 oz) can wild salmon, drained, bones & skin removed
  • 1/2 cup yellow onion, finely diced
  • 2 scallions, sliced
  • 1 large egg, beaten
  • 2 tbsp Dijon mustard
  • 1 tsp hot sauce (Crystal or Tabasco)
  • 1/2 cup almond flour or whole-wheat breadcrumbs
  • 1 tsp Old Bay or smoked paprika
  • 1 tbsp olive oil for pan
  • Bunch of kale or collards, sliced (to serve)

Lunch · Asma Khan's Chicken Jhal Farezi

Khan runs Darjeeling Express in Soho, London, and was the first British chef on Netflix's Chef's Table. Jhal Farezi was invented by Bengali cooks to use up leftover roast meat — aggressively spiced, dry-fried with peppers and onion. Skip the Indian-restaurant cream/sugar finish; Khan's version doesn't need either.

Ingredients
  • 500g chicken thigh, cut into strips (or shredded leftover roast)
  • 2 onions, sliced
  • 1 green bell pepper, sliced
  • 2–3 green chilies, slit
  • 1-inch ginger, julienned
  • 4 cloves garlic, sliced
  • 2 tomatoes, chopped
  • 1 tsp turmeric, 2 tsp ground cumin, 1 tsp ground coriander
  • 1/2 tsp Kashmiri chili powder
  • 1 tsp tomato paste
  • Mustard oil or olive oil
  • Cilantro to finish, lemon wedges

Serve with cauliflower rice or a single roti, not basmati.

Dinner · Yia Vang's Hmong-Inspired Grilled Chicken with Herb Salad

Vang runs Vinai in Minneapolis and is one of the best advocates for Hmong food in the country. His marinade leans on lemongrass, fish sauce, and bird's-eye chili — no sugar required. Pair with a giant Hmong herb salad (mint, cilantro, Thai basil, scallions, lime).

Ingredients
  • 1.5 lb chicken thighs, bone-in or boneless
  • 2 stalks lemongrass, white parts smashed and minced
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced
  • 2-inch piece ginger, grated
  • 2 bird's-eye or Thai chilies, minced
  • 3 tbsp fish sauce
  • 1 tbsp soy sauce
  • Juice of 1 lime
  • 1 tsp toasted sesame oil
  • Salad: big handful each cilantro, mint, Thai basil; 4 scallions; 1 cucumber, sliced; 1 jalapeño

Snack 1 · Hawa Hassan's Basbaas-Style Spicy Yogurt Dip + Crudités

Hassan grew up between Somalia, Kenya, and the US and built Basbaas around her mother's hot sauces. Stir her basbaas (or any quality green/red hot sauce) into Greek yogurt with garlic, cilantro, and lemon — high protein, zero added sugar, and far more interesting than hummus. Dip with whatever's in the fridge.

Ingredients
  • 1 cup whole-milk Greek yogurt (or 2% if cutting fat)
  • 2–3 tbsp basbaas green hot sauce (or substitute: zhug, schug, or 1 jalapeño + cilantro + garlic blended)
  • 1 small garlic clove, microplaned
  • Juice of 1/2 lemon
  • Pinch flaky salt
  • Cucumber spears, radishes, bell pepper strips, endive leaves to dip

Snack 2 · Padma Lakshmi's Spiced Roasted Chickpeas

From Lakshmi's Tangy, Tart, Hot & Sweet. Cumin, chili, smoked paprika, and lemon — the shelf-stable snack that crushes the 3pm crash without spiking blood sugar.

Ingredients
  • 1 (15 oz) can chickpeas, drained, dried thoroughly
  • 1 tbsp olive oil
  • 1 tsp ground cumin
  • 1 tsp smoked paprika
  • 1/2 tsp chili powder
  • 1/2 tsp garlic powder
  • 1/4 tsp ground cardamom
  • Salt to taste
  • Zest of 1 lemon (added after roasting)

Roast 425°F, 25–30 min, shaking pan halfway. Hit with lemon zest right out of the oven.
