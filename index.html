SAT-1 · Sugar Addiction Recovery

Saturday tends to stack itself — volunteering, a jam, a catering shift, errands. The pull toward sugar in the middle of a long active day isn’t weakness. It’s a habit loop: a body running low on stable fuel reaches for the fastest hit it can find, gets a brief spike, then crashes harder and reaches again. Dr. Anna Lembke at Stanford frames it cleanly — every dopamine spike (sugar, ultra-processed food, alcohol) is followed by a dip below baseline. The way out isn’t willpower. It’s interrupting the loop with steadier fuel up front and small counter-moves at the moment of the urge.

Tracker note: Supabase data wasn’t reachable from this run’s environment, so this section uses the general spec rather than personalized note patterns. If a craving pattern is showing up this week, the strategies below still apply — tailor where it lands.

1. Front-load 25–30g of protein before you leave the apartment.

A protein-anchored breakfast (eggs + sourdough, Greek yogurt + nuts, a smoothie with whey) flattens the spike-crash curve that drives mid-day sugar reaching. For the fatty liver, gout, and pre-diabetes stack, this is the single highest-leverage move you can make on a Saturday. Prep one thing tonight — overnight oats with nut butter, or hard-boil four eggs — so morning-John doesn’t have to decide.

2. Pack a “crash kit” for the bag, the car, the gig.

Two slots: (1) sustained-energy fuel — jerky or string cheese, a small bag of almonds, an apple, a protein bar under 8g of sugar; (2) a non-food dopamine bridge — sparkling water with lime, gum, or a quick cold-water splash on the wrists and face. Lembke’s research is clear that brief cold exposure produces a slow, sustained dopamine rise — the exact opposite of a sugar spike. When the 3pm wall hits between the volunteer shift and the jam, you reach into the bag, not into the pastry case at the catering staging area.

3. At the moment of the craving: urge-surf for 10 minutes.

ACT and Mindfulness-Based Relapse Prevention research is consistent: cravings peak and fall within 20–30 minutes if you don’t feed them. When the pull hits, name it out loud (“there’s the craving — this is the loop, not me”), set a 10-minute timer, drink 16oz of water, and do one small physical thing — walk a block, carry a load, tune a drumhead. The defusion move is the whole point: it’s a wave moving through, not a command you have to obey.

Today’s nudge

The pull toward sugar today isn’t weakness — it’s your brain trying to borrow energy it thinks you don’t have. Get the protein in before you leave, keep the crash kit within arm’s reach, and when the craving hits between gigs, set a 10-minute timer and let the wave pass. Steadier fuel is what makes the long Saturday feel possible instead of impossible.

Mantra: Steady fuel, steady day. The wave passes if I let it.

SAT-3 · Book Chapter Summary

A People’s History of the United States by Howard Zinn — Chapter 18: “The Impossible Victory: Vietnam.”

Main thesis

Zinn reframes the Vietnam War not as a Cold War tragedy of strategic miscalculation but as a confrontation between “organized modern technology versus organized human beings” — in which the human beings won. The chapter argues the war ended because of two intertwined revolts: Vietnamese peasants resisting a foreign superpower, and an unprecedented American antiwar movement that constrained Washington from within.

Key events, figures, and arguments

  • Traces U.S. involvement from 1946, when America bankrolled most of France’s colonial war in Indochina, through the fall of Saigon. Leaked memos showing concern over Southeast Asia’s tin, rubber, and oil sit alongside the public anti-communist rationale.
  • Centers Ho Chi Minh and the Vietnamese independence movement as nationalists invoking the U.S. Declaration of Independence — complicating the “communist aggression” story Washington told the public.
  • Documents the Gulf of Tonkin incident, the Pentagon Papers, My Lai, and the systematic gap between official statements and internal government assessments.
  • Highlights GI resistance — fragging, desertion, underground newspapers, Vietnam Veterans Against the War — arguing soldier disaffection reached a scale unseen in prior American wars.
  • Treats the antiwar movement (SDS, draft resistance, clergy, Black Power critiques linking imperialism abroad to racism at home) as a decisive political force, not a footnote to elite decision-making.

Why this chapter is relevant in June 2026

With renewed debate over U.S. military commitments, drone and proxy warfare, and campus protest movements being met with police crackdowns, Zinn’s chapter reads as a near-template for understanding how official war narratives erode and how dissent reshapes foreign policy. His insistence on counting Vietnamese and protester voices as historical actors — not background noise — speaks directly to contemporary arguments about whose casualties and whose speech count in democratic deliberation.
