WED-3App Dev: AI, Backend & Tools

1. Prompt caching is the single biggest lever on your Claude bill — and the TTL changed

What it is. Mark the stable parts of your prompt (system instructions, Buddy persona, recent journal context) as cache_control. The first call pays a write premium (~1.25×); subsequent calls inside the TTL pay ~10% of normal input cost.

Why it matters. Chat-style apps repeat the same system prompt and history on every turn — without caching you're paying full price to re-read the same tokens. Anthropic dropped the default cache TTL from 60 minutes to 5 minutes earlier this year; many teams saw bills jump 30–60% silently. You can opt into the 1-hour TTL (costs 2× to write, breaks even after two reads).

For John's Tracker. In the Buddy Edge Function, put the persona + last N journal entries inside a cache_control block. Pick the 1-hour TTL if a typical chat burst is > 5 minutes. Daily briefing won't benefit (one-shot per day); Buddy will.

Source: Claude Prompt Caching in 2026 — the 5-minute TTL change

2. The "hub" Edge Function pattern: fewer, bigger functions beat many tiny ones

What it is. Supabase's current guidance is to develop a small number of large Edge Functions ("hubs") that internally route to features, instead of one function per endpoint. Combine with a deny-by-default allowlist and a USER_DATA block convention to defend against prompt injection.

Why it matters. Each function is its own cold-start surface, its own deploy, and its own place to forget an auth check. Consolidating cuts cold starts, makes shared helpers (auth, logging, Claude client) reusable, and gives you one place to audit security. The _shared folder convention is the supported way to share code without deploying it.

For John's Tracker. Consider one ai Edge Function that dispatches buddy.chat, briefing.generate, entry.summarize internally — one shared Claude client, one auth check, one rate limiter.

Source: Supabase Edge Functions — Development Tips

3. Automatic embeddings via pgmq + pg_cron — stop running your own pipeline

What it is. A documented Supabase pattern: a Postgres trigger drops a job into pgmq (Postgres message queue) when a row changes, pg_cron drains the queue and calls an Edge Function that generates the embedding and writes it back. HNSW is the 2026 default index (faster queries, slightly slower writes than IVFFlat).

Why it matters. Embedding inline on insert couples your write path to an external API's latency and uptime. A separate worker is one more thing for an indie to host. Doing it inside Postgres means failures retry automatically, the queue is visible in SQL, and there's nothing extra to deploy.

For John's Tracker. When you start semantic search over tracker entries (or letting Buddy recall "what was I thinking about last month?"), wire it: trigger → pgmq → cron → Edge Function → embedding column. Then ORDER BY embedding <=> query_embedding LIMIT 5 to feed Buddy relevant memory.

Source: Supabase Automatic Embeddings docs

4. The agentic loop: keep one growing messages array — don't rebuild it

What it is. A real tool-using Claude agent is a while loop: send messages, check stop_reason; if it's tool_use, run the tool, append the result to the same messages array, send again. Stop when stop_reason is no longer tool_use. The discipline: append, never rebuild.

Why it matters. Rebuilding from scratch loses context Claude was relying on and burns tokens re-establishing state. Treating messages as an append-only log is what makes multi-turn tool use work — and it's what makes prompt caching effective, since the prefix stays stable.

For John's Tracker. When Buddy gains tools (search_entries, create_entry, set_reminder), structure the Edge Function as this loop. Pair with #1 — the growing array is exactly what caching is built for. Use Sonnet for main reasoning, Haiku for "classify this / extract that" sub-calls.

Source: Claude API tool use & agentic workflows

5. Background jobs without a job runner: tables + Edge Functions + cron

What it is. A pattern documented by indie builder Jignesh: a jobs table, an Edge Function that processes a batch, and pg_cron to invoke on a schedule. No Redis, no BullMQ, no separate worker.

Why it matters. Daily briefing generation is the textbook "I need a background job" case — but the textbook answer (Redis + a worker) is overkill for one developer. Tables give you durability (jobs survive restarts), observability (SQL queries the queue), and retries (just don't mark the row done).

For John's Tracker. The daily briefing generator wants to be exactly this. A briefing_jobs table, cron at e.g. 5 AM user-local inserting rows for active users, an Edge Function pulling a batch, calling Claude, writing the briefing, marking complete. Failures stay in the queue and get retried the next day without code.

Source: How I solved background jobs using Supabase tables and Edge Functions

WED-5Passive Income

This week's angles: real-estate adjacent, licensing/IP, automated services, creator-economy niches. Honest numbers, real catches.

1. Drum sample packs on Splice / Loopmasters (licensing/IP — direct skill fit)

What it is. Record original drum loops, one-shots, and grooves; license them through a marketplace. Splice pays per-sample royalties; Loopmasters/Sellfy pay per-pack. Once a pack is live, income trickles for years with zero further work.

Honest setup & income. 40–80 hours to record/edit/tag a quality 50–100-sound pack. Realistic: $50–$200/mo per pack after a moderate launch; 5–10 packs in a catalog reports out at $200–$2,000/mo. Splice-accepted packs skew higher (one Jamaican producer's first Splice pack 4×'d his prior annual income in 3 months). Top earners ($5K–$20K/mo) are outliers, usually with brand/audience.

The catch. Splice has a curation gate — most submissions get rejected. Self-publishing on Loopmasters/Sellfy means you do the marketing. The trap/hip-hop kit market is oversaturated; your edge is real acoustic drumming, which is underserved. No upfront capital beyond gear you already own.

Source: Orphiq — Sample Pack Sales: A Producer's Revenue Guide

2. Neighbor.com — rent out storage / parking space (real-estate adjacent, only if you control space)

What it is. Peer-to-peer Airbnb for unused parking, driveways, garages, basements, sheds. Renter pays monthly; Neighbor handles billing and provides $1M host liability.

Honest setup & income. ~2 hours to list. NYC parking spots run $50–$300/mo; garages $50–$500/mo. One Reddit host renting 6 RV/boat spots makes ~$1,000/mo. Realistic top end for one NYC parking spot in Brooklyn/Queens: $250–$400/mo net after Neighbor's ~4.9% + transaction fee.

The catch. Requires actually owning/controlling a deeded spot, driveway, or basement — most Astoria renters don't. Leases almost universally prohibit subletting storage. NYC zoning and co-op/condo boards frequently ban it. Skip unless you have a deeded garage/driveway or a homeowner friend to partner with on revenue share.

Source: Side Hustle Nation — Neighbor.com Review (real host interviews)

3. Fundrise (REIT-style) — honest reality check (capital allocation, not a side hustle)

What it is. Pooled real-estate fund; $10 minimum to start. Quarterly dividends + appreciation. Not really "passive income" — it's a portfolio allocation marketed as one.

Honest setup & income. 15 minutes to set up. Long-term net return ~7% annualized — but annual swings have ranged –7.45% to +22.99%. On $10K invested, expect ~$700/yr in good years, losses in bad ones. Arrived Homes (the single-family alternative) averaged just 3.9% dividend yield in 2025, with individual properties ranging 0.4%–10.4% — you can pick a dud.

The catch. Requires real upfront capital ($10K+ to mean anything). Liquidity is locked — 1% redemption penalty under 5 years, and Fundrise paused/queued redemptions for months in 2022–23. Not an emergency fund. For comparison: Vanguard's VNQ REIT ETF is fully liquid with lower fees. Fundrise's main advantage is psychological (feels like "real" real estate), not financial.

Source: Moneywise — Arrived Homes Review: tested with $1,000

4. Notion templates for enterprise IT / DevOps niches (creator economy — leverages 12 yrs IT)

What it is. Build a Notion template (runbooks, incident response, on-call rotation, SOC2 evidence collector, sprint planner for IT teams) and sell on Gumroad / Notion Marketplace / Etsy. Buyers duplicate it into their workspace.

Honest setup & income. 20–60 hours per polished template + landing page. Realistic newbie: $100–$300/mo first month with 1–2 listings. After 6 months with 5–10 templates: $1,000–$3,000/mo. Outlier earners (Easlo at $20K/mo, Thomas Frank's $1M+ on two templates) had massive existing audiences — don't anchor on them.

The catch. The generic productivity/habit-tracker space is saturated and dead. Your edge is the enterprise IT vertical (incident postmortems, change management, vendor security review trackers) — corporate buyers have budget, not broke students. Requires ongoing marketing. AI-generated competitor templates are flooding the market in 2026; differentiation has to come from real domain expertise — which you have, painfully.

Source: Kajabi — Can You Make Money Selling Notion Templates? (creator earnings breakdown)

Quick verdict
  • Highest skill-fit, lowest risk: Drum sample packs (#1). Real acoustic drumming is a moat in a synthetic-heavy market.
  • Best capital-light leverage: Notion templates in the enterprise IT niche (#4). Your 12 years of war stories are the differentiator.
  • Skip: Neighbor (#2), unless you have a deeded NYC parking spot.
  • Treat as portfolio allocation, not income: Fundrise (#3) — and honestly compare to VNQ first.
