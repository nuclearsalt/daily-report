SAT-1 · Sugar Addiction Recovery

This week you named it yourself: "I think it's the sugar. The fucking sugar." And in a more honest moment on Monday: "I might need sugar in order to do that effectively. Because I'm exhausted." That's The Cycle in one sentence — the belief that sugar is the only on-ramp to capability, when it's actually the thing tearing the engine apart.

The neuroscience here is not a moral failure story. Dr. Anna Lembke (Stanford addiction medicine, Dopamine Nation) describes it as the pleasure–pain balance: the brain restores equilibrium after a dopamine spike by tipping an equal amount in the opposite direction. You don't return to baseline after the chocolate-covered cherries — you dip below it. That dip is the crash, the fatigue, the "I literally can't move" feeling that shows up an hour later. And the brain's solution is to crave the next spike, even though the next spike is also the cause of the next crash. Lembke on NPR Life Kit.

You're not failing at willpower. You're trapped in a feedback loop with sound biological mechanics. The way out is to remove the loop's fuel for long enough that baseline dopamine starts recovering — Lembke's clinical observation is that this takes roughly two to four weeks, not days. The first week is the worst. The third week is when people start noticing they have steady energy for the first time in years.

Saturday context: you've got social plans tomorrow you've already committed to (good — your evening note today: "I have social things to do tomorrow and damnit I need to go and do those social things!"). Today and tomorrow are long active days. Three concrete strategies that fit that reality:

1. Pre-decide tomorrow's first meal tonight.

The cycle hijacks decision-making when blood sugar is low and energy is fried — i.e., the moment you wake up. If breakfast is already chosen and the ingredients are in the kitchen, you bypass the decision your tired brain will lose. Tofu (you cooked some Tuesday — still good), eggs with greens, or oats with peanut butter and berries. Protein + fiber + fat = no spike, no crash. The decision is the hard part; the cooking is mechanical.

2. Treat the 3 PM energy dip as a signal, not a sugar invitation.

The dip is real and predictable. It's not a deficiency of cookies. It's the body asking for two things: water and a brief state change. Try the sequence: 16 oz water → 5 minutes of any movement (walk around the block, two minutes on the drum kit, even just stairs) → a protein-forward snack (handful of nuts, hard-boiled egg, jerky, hummus on celery). The crash you'd otherwise get from the cookies costs you the whole next hour. The protein snack costs you nothing.

3. Plan the social re-entry around food you actually want to eat.

Lucille's on the calendar. The jam. The catering work when it comes. The thing that wrecked Wednesday wasn't choosing to go to El Kallejón — it was that you were so hungry by the time you got there that ordering both specials felt fine. Eat something substantial 90 minutes before any social food situation. You walk in hungry-but-not-starving and your decisions get made by you, not by the dopamine deficit.

Today's nudge The Cycle isn't a character flaw — it's a feedback loop with biology behind it, and feedback loops break when you starve them of one ingredient for long enough. You don't have to fix everything today. You have to not feed it once, then not feed it again. Today's a long day with social things tomorrow. Get the protein in early, keep water on you, and when the 3 PM "I need sugar to function" voice shows up, name it: that's the deficit talking, not me. The energy you're chasing is on the other side of three weeks of not chasing it.
Mantra for today: "The energy I want is on the other side of the spike I'm refusing."

SAT-3 · Book Chapter Summary

This week: William Blum, Killing Hope: US Military and CIA Interventions Since World War II

Chapter: "Indonesia 1965 — Liquidating President Sukarno… and 500,000 others" (Ch. 31)

Country/region: Indonesia (with a coda on East Timor, 1975)
Time period: 1965–1966 primarily, with consequences extending through Suharto's 32-year "New Order"

Main thesis

Blum argues that having failed to topple the non-aligned nationalist Sukarno through the 1958 covert war in Sumatra and Sulawesi, the U.S. succeeded in 1965 by quietly enabling General Suharto and the Indonesian Army to seize power and physically annihilate the Indonesian Communist Party (PKI) — then the world's largest non-ruling communist party. The mass slaughter of 500,000 to 1 million suspected leftists was not a CIA-orchestrated coincidence but an outcome Washington actively encouraged, facilitated, and celebrated as a Cold War victory in Southeast Asia.

Key events & arguments

  • The September 30, 1965 "Untung coup" — a murky kidnapping and killing of six army generals blamed on the PKI — gave Suharto's faction the pretext to crush the party. Blum and later declassified cables suggest U.S. officials knew the PKI link was thin but amplified it anyway.
  • The U.S. embassy under Ambassador Marshall Green supplied propaganda guidance ("spread the story of PKI's guilt, treachery, and brutality"). Per CIA officer Robert Martens, the embassy handed the Indonesian army lists of thousands of PKI cadres who were subsequently hunted down and killed.
  • Material support flowed via covert channels: communications equipment, small arms, medicine for the army, and continued military training relationships built up over years — even as overt aid to Sukarno was cut.
  • Long-running economic and diplomatic pressure (IMF/World Bank squeezes, withdrawn aid) plus cultivation of the officer class as a parallel power center set the structural stage for the takeover.
  • The bloodletting installed Suharto's "New Order," reopened Indonesia to Western oil, mining (Freeport), and timber capital. U.S. officials and media — Time, James Reston — repeatedly described it as "a gleam of light in Asia."
  • The chapter's coda on East Timor (1975): Ford and Kissinger gave Suharto a green light to invade the day before the assault, producing another ~200,000 deaths with U.S.-supplied weapons.

Why it's relevant today

The 2017 release of U.S. embassy cables by the National Security Archive vindicated much of Blum's argument, showing Washington tracked and welcomed the killings in real time — a reminder that "intelligence failure" framings often obscure deliberate policy. The episode is a template still visible in current U.S. behavior: backing friendly militaries against elected left-leaning governments, treating mass atrocities as acceptable costs of anti-leftist consolidation, and shielding successor regimes from accountability. Indonesia's unresolved 1965 reckoning — no truth commission, perpetrators still honored — mirrors impunity patterns the U.S. has tolerated from Guatemala to the Philippines. It also frames current debates about U.S. security assistance to authoritarian partners (Egypt, Saudi Arabia, the Philippines under Marcos Jr.), where the 1965 logic of "stability over democracy" persists.
