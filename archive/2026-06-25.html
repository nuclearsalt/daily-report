THU-1 Sugar Addiction Recovery

You logged a real win last night: "did not eat any sweets so far today. Win!" (6/25 quick check-in). That's the first crack in The Cycle this week — and it's worth naming what you're up against so it doesn't get smoothed over.

Here's the loop that's been pulling you under, in your own words from this week:

  • 6/23 midday — "anger at everything … to cope, I once again did the stupid fucking thing I always do: eat high-sugar foods. Two chocolate bars today."
  • 6/23 quick — "I don't know how I'm going to crawl out of this … I need to get out of The Cycle and I do not know how to make that happen."
  • 6/24 morning — "battling the low energy demons for seemingly the past month."
  • 6/25 evening — "Maybe I can keep going on sweets abstention, and have my energy level out, and then have the energy to unclutter the room."

You named the mechanism yourself in that last note. The loop: anger or low energy → high-sugar food for a 30-min spike → crash to lower-than-baseline energy → feel incapable → reach for the next quick fix. Dr. Anna Lembke calls this the dopamine seesaw — every spike is paid for with a deeper dip, and your brain remembers the spike and forgets the dip. The chocolate bars on 6/23 didn't cause the energy crash on 6/24; they compounded it.

This is a habit loop, not a character flaw. "I once again did the stupid fucking thing I always do" is a habit talking. The same brain that learned that loop can unlearn it — but only by interrupting the trigger, not by white-knuckling the craving.

Three strategies for today

  1. Pre-decide the protein, not the prohibition. "I won't eat sweets" is a will-power tax that you pay every time the thought appears. "I will eat 30g of protein before noon" is a single decision that crowds out the trigger. Tofu, eggs, Greek yogurt, leftover chicken — whatever's already in the fridge. Pick it now, before low-energy-you has to decide.
  2. Name the trigger out loud the moment it fires. When you feel that pull — and you will today — say to yourself "this is the cycle" before you say anything else. That tiny pause moves the urge from automatic behavior into the prefrontal cortex where you actually have a vote. Lembke calls it "the pause." It works because addiction lives in the gap between trigger and action; if you widen the gap by even 10 seconds, you often choose differently.
  3. Replace the spike, not the energy. When the urge hits, the brain wants fast pleasure, not nutrition. Cold water on the face, two minutes outside, a song from the kit, ice in a glass with a splash of something tart — anything that delivers a fast sensory hit without sugar. You're not pretending it's the same; you're showing your nervous system that the spike can come from somewhere else.
Today's nudge

Last night you wrote that you're exhausted just thinking about uncluttering the living room. Read that note back — the path you described is right. Level the energy first, then the cluttered room gets handled by a calmer, more capable version of you. Yesterday you proved you can choose differently for one day. Today is just one more day. Stack one more.

Mantra: One spike costs me tomorrow. I'm keeping tomorrow.

THU-2 Music Production: Home Studio Techniques

Tuned to your rig: Roland TD-11, Focusrite 2i2, Logic Pro (Mac + iPad), Ample Metal Hellrazer, GGD Modern & Massive 2, Steven Slate Drums 5, Splice + NI libraries.

1. Save undo history with your project (Logic Pro 11.2)

Logic Pro 11.2 added a Settings → General option that persists undo history across save/close. Because the full edit history reopens with the project, you can revisit a drum edit or guitar comp from days ago without rebuilding it — huge for songs that get tracked across many sessions.

For your rig: Flip it on before the next songwriting session on the Mac. Pairs well with Logic Remote on the iPad — record a TD-11 take from the kit, then roll back any pass later without losing the trail.

Logic Pro Help — 11.2 new features

2. Keep MIDI drum humanization ≤ 8% (velocity AND timing)

Programmed-drum rule of thumb that keeps holding up: humanize beyond ~8% on either velocity or timing and it starts to sound sloppy rather than human. Real drummers have tight micro-variation, not loose macro-variation — small randomization reads as feel, large reads as drunk.

For your rig: When you write SSD5 parts in the piano roll (double-kick patterns, fills you haven't dialed in physically yet), cap humanize at 8% and only write what a real drummer could play. Respects your 20-year ear for what's executable.

Mastering.com — Program realistic MIDI drums

3. Blend two IRs inside GGD Modern & Massive 2 instead of one

Modern amp-sim mixing has moved away from a single cab IR. The go-to is slot 1 = focused close-mic IR (SM57-style) for midrange bite, slot 2 = room IR (e.g. U87) for space and depth. Stacked, you get the cut of a close mic with the three-dimensionality of a real tracking room.

For your rig: Inside M&M2's mixer, run a close+room IR blend on rhythm guitars. Bonus: borrow GGD's drum-library room IRs onto guitars to glue them into the same space as SSD5 — a free move since you already own GGD products.

Nail the Mix — depth with GGD room IRs

4. HPF + 250–350 Hz cut to carve DI bass around guitars

When DI bass (Hellrazer) and rhythm guitars (GGD M&M2) fight, the cleanest fix is: HPF guitars at 80–100 Hz for standard tuning or 60–70 Hz for drop tunings, then cut 2–4 dB at 250–350 Hz on whichever source is muddier. That clears the low-mid blanket so the bass fundamental and the guitar chug each get their own lane.

For your rig: Hellrazer has a roughly 50 ms pick-attack delay by default — set a –50 ms region nudge or track delay in Logic so the bass locks to your kick.

Komposition101 — Guitar & bass frequencies for metal

5. Track Stacks + Track Alternatives for fast song iteration

Track Stacks fold a drum kit or backing-vocal group under one fader — you mix the section, not 12 channels. Track Alternatives let you keep multiple takes or arrangement ideas (different chorus, different solo) on a single track without duplicating it. Together you A/B arrangement decisions in seconds.

For your rig: SSD5 multi-out routing and layered guitars (GGD plus Splice/NI sweeteners) collapse into one DRUMS and one GTRS stack. Track Alternatives = try a 7/8 pre-chorus vs. a 4/4 one without losing either.

Loopmasters — Logic Pro workflow tips

6. Write one odd-meter section per song

A reliable songwriting move right now: a single 5/4 or 7/8 passage (often pre-chorus or bridge) inside an otherwise 4/4 song. The contrast creates tension that the chorus then resolves by returning to 4 — the listener feels the payoff without consciously knowing why.

For your rig: Sketch the odd-meter riff on guitar, then track it live on the TD-11 so the part stays human and playable. A Kontakt pad under the odd-meter section marks it as a "moment."

Develop Device — Modern metal songwriting

THU-3 Music Sight-Reading for Drummers

You play fluently but didn't grow up reading rhythmic notation. Reading is a stamina skill — the only way in is daily, low-tempo reps where you never have to stop. Here's a small library to actually do it from.

Books (2–3)

Progressive Steps to Syncopation for the Modern Drummer — Ted Reed

The standard reference. Page 38 alone has been used by pros for decades — each one-bar rhythm can be applied dozens of ways (ride patterns, bass-drum figures, jazz comping, samba clave). Best book for tying reading to actual playing.

Amazon  ·  Hudson Music

Modern Reading Text in 4/4 — Louis Bellson & Gil Breines

Exercises are laid out like real charts — varied notation, rests, ties — closer to what you'll see on a session music stand than Reed. Best book specifically for sight-reading (read it once, move on) vs. deep interpretation.

Alfred Music  ·  Amazon

Stick Control for the Snare Drummer — George Lawrence Stone

Not strictly a reading book, but for someone who didn't grow up reading, it builds the eye–hand link with simple R/L notation. Use it as a warm-up reader before Reed or Bellson.

Drumeo — Books every drummer should own

Apps & software tools

Rhythm Sight Reading Trainer (ReadRhythm) — iOS, free

Generates endless random rhythmic exercises (2–12 beats per bar, common and odd meters). Tap on screen or via MIDI device (works with the TD-11 through USB). Best free tool for daily reps.

App Store

RhythmBot (by JP Bouvet) — web/iOS, free + paid

Made by a drummer specifically for drummers. Generates notation with adjustable complexity, subdivisions, and time signatures. Great for session-style "what's this bar?" practice.

Melodics — Top drum notation apps

Sight Reading Factory — web, ~$35/yr

Originally for melodic instruments, but the rhythm-only mode is gold. Generates fresh exercises every time so you can't memorize, with adjustable difficulty and meters — the closest thing to a session-chart simulator.

sightreadingfactory.com

Free blogs & videos

Cruise Ship Drummer! (Todd Bishop)

Working pro who writes extensively about reading, Reed interpretations, and sight-reading drills. Tons of free downloadable PDFs aimed at exactly your use case: sub gigs, charts.

cruiseshipdrummer.com — Bellson reading text post

Stephen Taylor Drums (YouTube + site)

Working sub who walks through how to read drum charts, road maps, and rehearsal notation. Search his channel for "chart reading" and "sight reading."

Stephen's Drum Shed

10–15 min daily routine
  1. Warm-up read (2 min): Open Stick Control to a random page. Play 4 lines at ♩ = 70 with eyes glued to the page. Don't memorize — turn the page if you catch yourself looking up.
  2. Reed page 38 (5 min): Pick one line. Pass 1: play the written rhythm on snare with jazz ride and hi-hat on 2 & 4. Pass 2: same rhythm on bass drum with swing on top. Different "voicing" each day.
  3. App rep (3–5 min): Open Rhythm Sight Reading Trainer or RhythmBot. Generate 4–8 fresh bars. Play through on a pad, no stops. Repeat once a click faster.
  4. Chart day (1×/week instead of step 3): Read a real chart cold — free big-band PDF or a fresh Bellson exercise. Goal: through it once without stopping, count out loud at rests.

Tempo low enough that you never have to stop. The win is unbroken flow, not perfect hits.

THU-4 Tips from World-Class Drummers

1. Stewart Copeland — "Practice perfection"

Genre: Rock (The Police). Distinct hi-hat work and reggae-inflected grooves.

The tip: Find the exact tempo at which you can execute a rudiment flawlessly — and never go above it during practice. Playing imperfectly above that speed only teaches your hands wrongness. Copeland also calls quiet playing "the most unappreciated technique" — drums sound better, body stays relaxed.

Why it's useful: Coming off a 6-year break, your muscle memory rebuilds cleanly only at tempos you can already execute. Resist pushing speed; reclaim chops at low volume first.

MusicRadar — Stewart Copeland's top 5 tips

2. Vinnie Colaiuta — "Thought is the enemy of flow"

Genre: Fusion / session (Sting, Frank Zappa, Jeff Beck).

The tip: Practice to build vocabulary so ideas flow freely — but don't let practice become a neurosis. Vinnie no longer practices obsessively; you need discipline, but constantly telling yourself "I have to practice" is counterproductive. Billy Cobham specifically told him to enjoy his practice time.

Why it's useful: Returning players guilt-trip themselves about lost ground. Vinnie's framing — build vocabulary off the kit, get out of your own way on the gig — is the headspace live performance demands.

VinnieColaiuta.com — Drum interview

3. Steve Gadd — Listen hard, leave space

Genre: Studio / R&B / jazz-pop (Steely Dan, Paul Simon, James Taylor). The most-recorded session drummer in history.

The tip: "A good musician is someone who listens real hard and leaves some space. If you have to fight for a space, it's not too much fun." Gadd says his best days are the ones he wakes up realizing he doesn't have the answers and has something to learn.

Why it's useful: For live performance after a long break, the temptation is to overplay to prove you're back. Listening and leaving space is the fastest way to make bandmates want to call you again.

Modern Drummer — Steve Gadd

4. Nate Smith — Don't watch the clock

Genre: Modern jazz / funk / pocket (Dave Holland, Fearless Flyers, Brittany Howard).

The tip: "If you're practicing and you're looking at the clock, you shouldn't be practicing." Practice should be playing. When you hit something hard, work on that specific thing. "Touch is huge" — your sound starts in the hands, not the gear.

Why it's useful: You don't need a structured 2-hour routine to get back in shape. Short sessions focused on what's actually broken — and rebuilding touch on your own kit — beat checkbox practice every time.

Drummer's Resource — Nate Smith

5. Mark Guiliana — Use ideas as your "North Star" + D.R.O.P.

Genre: Jazz / electronic / prog (Bowie's Blackstar, Brad Mehldau, Beat Music).

The tip: You only need enough technique to play your ideas. When you hear something in your head you can't yet execute, that gap is the practice goal. Use D.R.O.P. — Dynamics, Rate, Orchestration, Phrasing — and vary one element at a time over a single idea to unlock new vocabulary. Limited practice time (he has kids) actually sharpened his focus.

Why it's useful: Limited time + targeted work. D.R.O.P. extracts weeks of material from one groove or fill — perfect for a 45-year-old with a packed schedule.

Drummer's Review — Mark Guiliana

2. Original drum sample packs & loops on Splice + BeatStars

What it is: Record original drum loops, one-shots, fills, and kits from your home setup. Producers buy them as building blocks. Recurring per-stream/per-download royalties.

Realistic income: First-year solo creator with 1–3 packs: $20–$200/month. Producers with 5–10 well-marketed packs report $200–$2,000/month. The "$5k–$20k/month" numbers floating around are top-percentile creators with 30+ packs and audience — don't plan around them.

The catch: Splice gatekeeps via application form and curates aggressively — many first submissions get rejected. Every sample must be 100% original — no copyrighted breaks, no AI-generated content with murky rights, or your pack gets pulled.

Why it fits you: 20+ years on drums = the one skill that's hardest to fake. Most sample-pack drum loops sound stiff/quantized because they're made by producers, not drummers. Live-played loops by a real drummer are genuinely scarce.

What it is: Short instrumental beds (or drum-driven tracks) uploaded to non-exclusive sync libraries that license to YouTubers, podcasts, ads, TV. Each license = $5–$500+; popular tracks earn for years.

Why it fits you: Drum-forward instrumental beds (sports stingers, action podcast intros, tense documentary cues) are in constant demand and play to your strength. No vocals or melodies required.
