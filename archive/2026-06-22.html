MON-2 · Mindful Eating & Exercise for ADHD

The pattern this week is loud and clear: low energy → reach for sugar → small spike → crash → feel less capable → repeat. You named it on Saturday: "That was a weak excuse to eat sugar." ADHD makes this loop especially sticky because executive function tanks first when blood sugar swings, which is exactly when planning ahead feels impossible.

Three things to try this week

1. Pre-decide tomorrow's breakfast tonight. ADHD brains struggle most with decisions made in low-glucose, low-dopamine states (i.e. the morning). The decision itself is the executive-function tax — not the eating. Put a hard-boiled egg, a Greek yogurt, and a piece of fruit in a visible spot on the fridge shelf before bed. Tomorrow's you doesn't have to negotiate.

2. Replace the sugar reach with a protein-fat reach. When you feel the "low energy → I need a cookie" loop fire, the loop is real but the input is wrong. Sugar gives you 20 minutes of energy and takes 90 minutes back. A handful of almonds + a square of dark chocolate (85%+) feeds the craving without the crash. Keep the better option closer to you than the worse one.

3. Pair drum practice with the post-meal slump. You hit drums 4 of 8 days last week — your best activity. The 30–60 minutes after lunch is when energy crashes hardest. Schedule even 10 minutes of kit time there. Movement + dopamine + your strongest skill. Bonus: it solves "I should practice more" without needing willpower.

Today's nudge. You have labs to figure out today. Your past self has been writing notes all week that you trust the loop more than you trust the food. Today, you don't need to win the food fight all day — just pick the breakfast. One real meal before the labs, with protein. That's the whole assignment.
"Sugar borrows energy at a punishing interest rate."

Also worth noticing: ritalin has been skipped every single day this week (and the logs show you recorded the intended 1.25 mg dose, just didn't take it). ADHD nutrition strategies work harder when meds are absent. Worth a quick honest check-in with yourself about why the skip.

MON-7 · AI in IT: Workflow Automation

You're considering a return to enterprise IT. Below are five practical, currently-shipping AI techniques that cut the interruption load — the actual thing that makes IT draining. Ordered from "set up first" to "useful once you're settled."

1. LLM-as-scripting-partner (ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot)

What it does: Turns "I need a PowerShell script to find stale AD accounts and email the report" from 45 minutes into 5. Also parses log files, drafts regex, explains cryptic stack traces, converts manual runbooks into scripts.

Why it reduces burnout: The exhausting part of IT isn't the work — it's the context-switching when a ticket pulls you out of a half-written script and you lose your place. AI handles the boilerplate so your remaining focus goes to judgment calls. It also kills the "I know I've solved this before but where" tax.

Get started: A $20/month Claude or ChatGPT subscription on your personal account, or M365 Copilot if your employer provides one. Start by pasting in error messages. Graduate to "write me a PowerShell function that does X, comment every block." Always read what it gives you before running.

Limits: Hallucinates cmdlet names and flags — verify with Get-Help before running anything destructive. Never paste customer data, passwords, or internal hostnames into a public model.

Microsoft Copilot for IT admins

2. Self-service password reset (Microsoft Entra SSPR)

What it does: Lets users reset their own AD/Entra password from the lock screen after MFA verification. Eliminates the single most common L1 ticket.

Why it reduces burnout: Password resets are interruption-shaped — trivial individually, lethal to concentration. Killing this category is the highest-leverage hour you'll spend in your first month at a new shop.

Get started: If the org has Entra ID P1 or P2 (bundled with M365 Business Premium and up), SSPR is a config toggle, not a purchase. Pilot with one group, then expand.

Limits: Adoption is the hard part — users won't register methods unless you force registration via Conditional Access. The Sept 30, 2025 deprecation moved auth methods to the unified Authentication Methods policy; if you inherit a tenant on the legacy policy, migrate it first.

Microsoft Learn — Enable SSPR

3. AI ticket triage in the helpdesk platform (Freshservice, Atera)

What it does: Auto-categorizes tickets, routes them to the right queue, suggests priority, drafts a first reply from past resolved tickets, deflects common questions before they become a ticket.

Why it reduces burnout: You stop being a human router. Small service desks report ~30% fewer escalations, 15–20% better SLA — meaning fewer "why is this still open?" pings from managers.

Get started: If you're interviewing, ask what ticketing system the shop uses and whether AI add-ons are enabled. Freshservice Starter ~$19/agent/mo base; "Freddy AI" sits on higher tiers. Atera bundles AI Copilot into $149/mo-per-tech IT department plan (no per-endpoint charge — good for lean teams).

Limits: Pricing balloons when add-ons stack. AI suggestions need a few hundred historical tickets to be useful — greenfield instances are mediocre for the first quarter.

Flamingo — best AI helpdesk 2026 · Freshservice pricing 2026

4. AI-powered internal knowledge search (Guru, Glean, Notion AI)

What it does: Indexes SharePoint, Confluence, Slack, ticket history, runbooks. Answers "how do we provision a new contractor laptop?" in natural language with links to the source doc.

Why it reduces burnout: Tribal-knowledge anxiety is a huge component of returning-to-IT stress. Working internal search means you don't have to remember everything — and you don't have to interrupt a senior colleague to ask.

Get started: Cheapest path is Notion AI (~$10/user/mo) if the team uses Notion. Guru is safer for IT because cards require human verification — the AI won't confidently cite a stale 2019 runbook. Glean is enterprise-priced; usually a top-down purchase.

Limits: Garbage in, garbage out. If the docs are a mess, the AI will surface confidently-wrong answers. Plan a docs cleanup before deploying.

eesel — best AI KB tools 2026

5. Workflow automation for onboarding/offboarding (Tines, Power Automate)

What it does: Triggered by an HRIS event, the workflow creates the AD account, assigns licenses, provisions SaaS apps, ships the laptop. On termination it revokes everything with an audit trail. CISA's Jan 2025 rule now sets credential revocation within 10 days of termination as the regulatory bar — manual processes miss this.

Why it reduces burnout: Onboarding/offboarding is checklist-heavy, error-prone work where forgetting a step has heavy consequences (orphaned accounts, audit findings). Automating it removes both the tedium and the dread.

Get started: Power Automate is included with M365, lowest-friction starting point. Tines has a generous free Community Edition. Start with offboarding (lower risk if half-broken) before onboarding (where a broken flow = angry new hire on day one).

Limits: Workflow tools become load-bearing scripts nobody understands six months later. Document every flow.

Tines offboarding playbook · M365Scripts — Power Automate onboarding

Highest-leverage interview question. "What percentage of L1 tickets are password resets, and is SSPR deployed?" The answer tells you instantly whether the team is fighting fires they could have prevented — a strong signal of overall burnout culture.
