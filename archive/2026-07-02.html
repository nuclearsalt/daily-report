WED-1 · Mindful Eating & Exercise for ADHD

This week the data tells one clear story, and it's not about willpower. Energy sat low almost every day (mostly 3–5 out of 10), and the sweets showed up right alongside it — with "Low energy" logged as the reason on June 26, June 28, and again July 1. That's the loop, named plainly:

low energy → grab something sweet for a quick lift → short spike → crash → feeling like you can't do the thing → reach for the quick fix again.

Your June 30 note put it perfectly: "All I need to do is remove the sweets. Then we're golden. 'All I need to do.' As if it's just easy peasy lemon squeezy." Right — for an ADHD brain, "just remove it" isn't a plan, it's a wish. The brain reaching for sugar is doing exactly what it's wired to do: chase fast dopamine and fast glucose when the tank feels empty. So the strategies below aren't about resisting harder. They're about lowering the executive load so the better choice needs less brainpower to make.

Three things that actually work with an ADHD brain

1. Sequence beats motivation — and you already proved it this week

June 29 morning you ran a fixed order of operations: light yoga/weights → meds → breakfast → no coffee, no phone. The result, in your own words: "decluttered much of the living room, vacuumed, dishes… Didn't feel like a ton of effort… got more done than in the past few weeks. Just by taking my morning seriously." That wasn't a high-energy day — energy was a 5. Movement first, before decisions and before the phone, is what generated the capability. ADHD responds to structure and sequence, not to waiting until you feel like it. Rebuild that exact order tomorrow morning; it's a proven local win, dated and yours.

2. Pre-decide at the door, because the moment always wins otherwise

You wrote tonight: "I will be going to the market before they close… and I will have to keep myself honest." The honest truth about ADHD is that in-the-moment decisions lose to the dopamine pull almost every time — so don't leave the call for the moment. Make an implementation intention before you walk in: "protein and produce go in the basket first; I walk the perimeter, not the candy aisle." A decision made in advance is a decision your tired brain doesn't have to re-litigate under fluorescent lights at closing time. (Understood.org and ADDitude both lean hard on this — pre-commitment offloads the executive function you don't have to spare.)

3. Add protein before you subtract sugar

"Remove the sweets" is a subtraction task, and subtraction requires sustained restraint — the exact thing that's in short supply. Flip it to an addition task, which ADHD brains do better with: front-load protein and slow carbs (eggs, Greek yogurt, beans, chicken) before the craving window hits, usually mid-afternoon when energy craters. A fuller, slower-burning tank means the crash is smaller, which means the 4pm reach for sugar is quieter. You're not white-knuckling the candy away — you're making the craving show up smaller in the first place. Bonus, given the gout you flagged June 30: steadier blood sugar is also less fuel for a flare.

Today's nudge

You've had no sweets so far today — that's a real streak, not nothing. The market run tonight is the one pressure point, and you already saw it coming, which is half the battle. Before you leave: decide right now what goes in the basket, and eat something with protein before you go so you're not shopping on an empty, brain-foggy tank. You're not trying to be perfect at the market. You're trying to walk in with the decision already made, so the tired-brain version of you doesn't have to make it. More steady energy tomorrow = more capacity to knock out those audition songs. That's the trade, and it's worth it.

WED-2 · NYC Urban Planning & Global Urbanism

NYC

Mayor Mamdani's "Block by Block" housing plan May 2026

A decade-long roadmap to build 200,000 new affordable homes and preserve 200,000 existing affordable units, paired with roughly $5B over two years for new rent-stabilized housing (with senior and supportive-housing carve-outs) and a $22B five-year capital housing commitment. It also folds in tenant protections and a Construction Justice Act setting a $40/hr minimum plus benefits for workers on City-financed projects.

Who: Mayor Zohran Mamdani's administration. Impact: The broadest citywide affordability push in years — aimed at low-income renters and at raising labor standards on public construction.

nyc.gov — Block by Block announcement

Monitor Point — transit-oriented waterfront redevelopment, Greenpoint Jun 2026

A public-private deal converting an MTA depot on Quay Street (Greenpoint waterfront) into ~1,150 homes — 40% permanently affordable, most at 40–60% AMI — plus open space and community facilities. The developer funds a replacement MTA facility in East Williamsburg at no public cost and pays 100 years of rent to the MTA, money earmarked for signals, stations, and switch upgrades. The City Council approved the Monitor Point rezoning on June 25 (see WED-4).

Who: Gotham Organization (developer), MTA (landowner), local community board, City Planning Commission. Impact: Held up as a model of transit-oriented development that reinvests directly in the transit system rather than just building near it.

WED-3 · Radical Thinkers: Quotes & Summaries

Rosa Luxemburg

"Freedom only for the supporters of the government, only for the members of one party — however numerous they may be — is no freedom at all. Freedom is always the freedom of the one who thinks differently."

The Russian Revolution (written 1918, published posthumously 1922). Writing from prison, Luxemburg critiqued the Bolsheviks' suppression of political dissent, warning that a revolution which extinguishes free debate hollows out its own emancipatory promise. It endures as a left critique of authoritarianism from within the socialist tradition — liberty is meaningless unless it protects the person who disagrees.

Source

Frantz Fanon

"Colonialism is not satisfied merely with holding a people in its grip and emptying the native's brain of all form and content. By a kind of perverted logic, it turns to the past of the oppressed people, and distorts, disfigures, and destroys it."

The Wretched of the Earth (1961), from the chapter on national culture. Fanon argues colonial domination is not only economic and physical but psychological — an attempt to erase a people's history and sense of self. The passage remains central to postcolonial and decolonial thought about cultural reclamation.

Source (full text)

Michael Parenti

"In order that a select few might live in great opulence, millions of people have laboured all the days of their lives to make a bare living for themselves and often not even that."

Blackshirts and Reds (1997). Parenti condenses his materialist critique of capitalism: concentrated wealth isn't incidental to mass hardship but structurally dependent on it. The line is widely cited for compressing a systemic argument into a single moral indictment.

Source

Fred Hampton

"You can jail a revolutionary, but you can't jail the revolution. You can lock up a freedom fighter like Huey P. Newton, but you can't lock up freedom fighting."

Speech, 1969. Hampton, the young Chicago Black Panther leader, delivered this shortly before he was killed by police, framing individual repression as powerless against a broader movement. It endures as a statement about the durability of collective struggle beyond any single leader.

Source

James Baldwin

"Not everything that is faced can be changed, but nothing can be changed until it is faced."

"As Much Truth As One Can Bear," New York Times Book Review (January 14, 1962). Baldwin wrote this in an essay on the writer's obligation to confront hard realities rather than flatter the reader. Often misattributed to The Fire Next Time, it has become a touchstone for the idea that honest reckoning is the precondition for any real change.
