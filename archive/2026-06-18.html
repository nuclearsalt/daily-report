1 · ADHD: Exercise & Mindful Eating

Three angles to try this week, all built around the way ADHD brains actually work — low baseline dopamine, weak future-reward pull, weak self-cueing.

Strategy 1 — Stack the cue, not the willpower

Executive dysfunction isn't fixed by deciding harder. It's fixed by making the next action absurdly visible. Leave the gym shorts on the floor next to the bed. Put the water bottle on the laptop. The ADHD brain follows whatever's most obvious in the visual field, so put the right thing there before tomorrow's you has to choose. (ADDitude)

Strategy 2 — Eat for dopamine, not for restriction

Restrictive eating fails fast on ADHD brains because it removes a primary self-soothing input without replacing the reward. Lean into tyrosine-rich foods (eggs, salmon, chicken, tofu, pumpkin seeds, beans, almonds) which support dopamine synthesis directly — your body gets the chemistry it's already chasing, just from food instead of from chaos. Pair with a 20-second breath pause before the first bite to introduce the "pause" that mindful eating depends on. (ADDitude — Healthy Eating Habits, ADDitude — Nutrition)

Strategy 3 — Body-double + 5-minute rule for the start

Body doubling — working or moving with another person present (in person, on a video call, or via apps like Flow Club) — externalises the focus your prefrontal cortex isn't supplying. Combine with the 5-minute rule: commit to five minutes only. Research from the Center for ADHD Awareness shows 72% of ADHD users who start at five minutes extend into longer blocks. Five minutes of bike pedaling is easier to start than "a workout." (Cleveland Clinic, ADHD Wisdom Tools)

Today's nudge

Pick one cue to pre-stage tonight before you sleep. Put your bike helmet on the doorknob, or your sticks on top of the laptop. Don't promise yourself anything about tomorrow — just stage the cue. Your morning brain will do what's easy to see.

2 · Music Production: Home Studio Techniques

Tips matched to your actual rig — TD-11, Scarlett 2i2, SM58, guitar/bass DI, Logic Pro, Ample Metal Hellrazer, GGD Modern & Massive 2, SSD5, Splice, NI.

1 · Velocity is the difference between programmed and played

Even with a TD-11 firing real MIDI velocities, a quick pass through programmed fills will reveal grid-locked snare hits. The single biggest realism upgrade in GGD Modern & Massive 2 or SSD5 is varying ghost-note velocities by ±15 from the main backbeat and nudging the timing of 16th-note ghosts by ±10 ticks. Don't humanise the downbeats — humanise the ornaments. (Mastering.com, Splice blog)

2 · The Logic Pro "select all, gain -10 dB" reset

When tracks from Hellrazer or SSD5 print hot, don't fight it with the fader. Select all drum regions, open the Region Inspector, set Gain to -10 dB. Faders sit at zero, headroom returns, and the rest of the mix doesn't have to dive. Standard pro-engineer workflow, criminally underused in home setups. (Sound On Sound)

3 · Parallel-compress your drum bus inside Logic

Send the drum stack to a new aux, drop Logic's Studio FET Compressor on it (10:1 ratio, fast attack, fast release, 10–15 dB of gain reduction). Blend at 30–40%. Hellrazer kits especially come alive with this — the parallel comp keeps the transients pristine on the dry bus while the squashed bus adds body and weight underneath. (Logic Studio Training)

4 · Vocal chain on the SM58: gain → EQ → comp → de-ess

For voiceover work specifically, this order matters. Set input gain so peaks hit -6 dBFS on the 2i2. Channel EQ: roll off below 80 Hz, gentle 2 kHz boost for intelligibility. Compressor (Studio FET, 3:1, slow attack ~10 ms, fast release). DeEsser 2 last to tame any post-comp sibilance the SM58's presence peak introduces. Then send to a separate bus for reverb so the dry track stays clean. (Baywood Audio, Sage Audio)

5 · Arrange "subtractively" — kill, don't add

The instinct with Splice + NI + virtual drums is to keep layering until the verse has 14 tracks. The pro move is the inverse: write the chorus full, then for the verses, mute tracks until just the spine remains. Most modern arrangements live or die on the contrast between empty verses and full choruses. Mark/color-code sections in Logic before you arrange — "if it looks clear, it sounds clear." (MusicRadar, The Songwriter)

6 · Worth exploring: stem-separation as a writing tool

Moises (mobile + desktop) lets you upload a track and pull the drum stem out cleanly. Drop a song you love into it, mute the drums, then play along on the TD-11 — instant play-along practice for sight-reading and for studying arrangements. Also useful when transcribing a part you can't quite hear. (Moises)

3 · Sight-Reading for Drummers

For an experienced drummer whose reading lags the playing. The goal: convert what your hands already do into chart-readable shorthand, fast.

Books

Syncopation — Ted Reed

$10–14 · the standard since 1958

The book every studio drummer uses. Designed as a sight-reading exercise but applicable to anything — interpret the snare line on the ride, on the bass drum, as a samba, as a jazz comp. Lifetime book.

Alfred Music — Syncopation

Modern Reading Text in 4/4 — Louis Bellson & Gil Breines

$14 · the other standard

Pure rhythmic reading, no kit choreography. Perfect for the "I can play but I freeze in front of a chart" problem — strips the kit out so the eye learns the rhythms cold.

Alfred Music — Modern Reading Text

Big Band Drumming at First Sight — Steve Fidyk

$22 · Alfred Music · play-along audio included

If you want to ever sub on a big-band gig (one of the better-paying NYC drum scenes), this is the on-ramp. Ten real chart arrangements with audio, plus the reading conventions specific to big-band charts (figures, ensemble hits, breaks).

Alfred Music — Big Band Drumming at First Sight

The Drummer's Link to Sight Reading — Jennings & Ballinger

$25 · with online audio

Specifically about studio chart reading — slash notation, kicks, figures, dynamics, road maps. Most reading books treat drums as a snare instrument; this one treats drums as a kit on a session.

Free online

  • Patrick Blakley's free sight-reading booklet — 14-page PDF, six progressive levels, 48 graded passages. Zero cost, well-designed.
  • Learn Drums For Free — sight reading tag — Theo Lawrence's blog, focused articles and short exercises.
  • Percussive Arts Society — Sight-Reading Tips — short, professional-level rules-of-thumb.

A practice routine that actually works

  • 10 min/day, 5 days/week. More is not better — reading fatigue sets in fast.
  • Two passes: Pass 1 — eyes only, no sticks, count out loud. Pass 2 — play, no stopping for mistakes.
  • Never re-play a passage you've already read. Always advance. The skill is reading new material, not memorising old.
  • Metronome at 60 BPM the first week. Tempo earns its way up.

4 · Tips from World-Class Drummers

Steve Gadd — on serving the song

"Technique doesn't mean shit if you can't play a backbeat in a place that fits and lock it in." — Steve Gadd, on what session work taught him

Gadd keeps a kit set up at all times and practices in five-minute bursts whenever they appear — sometimes on the arm of a couch with no sticks. The lesson: drop the idea of a "practice session." Treat the kit as something you visit, not something you schedule. (DrumsTheWord — Gadd practice notes, PAS profile)

Vinnie Colaiuta — on flow

"Thought is the enemy of flow. There are no mistakes — only creative events." — Vinnie Colaiuta, Modern Drummer 2013

Colaiuta was famously the most obsessive practicer of his generation — pad in the car on the way to sessions — but said the breakthrough came when he gave himself permission not to practice for days at a time. The over-practice was a neurosis; the looseness was the music. Worth holding in mind when ADHD makes "I didn't practice today" feel like failure. (Modern Drummer 2013 interview, DRUM! Magazine)

Questlove — on emulation and the "sloppy meter"

"I made the meter sloppy as hell, but not so sloppy that you couldn't feel it." — Questlove, on his J Dilla-influenced pocket

Questlove's actionable advice: pick a drummer you love, learn their playing well enough to forge their feel, then move on to the next one. He emulated James Brown's drummers, Sly Stone's drummers, Dilla's programming. The catalog of feels you can summon comes from years of intentional imitation — not from "finding your own voice" in a vacuum. (Red Bull Music Academy — Give the Drummer Some, Soundfly)

Stewart Copeland — on not being just a drummer

"Drummers shouldn't just think of themselves as drummers. If you're going to be a musician, you should expand your horizons, compose things, and work with other instruments." — Stewart Copeland

Copeland was self-taught, never had a formal teacher, and built his style by absorbing Middle Eastern, African, and Latin rhythms from his upbringing. His advice for adding depth: practice polyrhythms between hands and feet, and treat syncopation as the primary engine — accent the offbeats first, the downbeats second. (Drumeo — Why Copeland is a genius)

Tony Williams — on clarity

"When you play something, for it to sound like what you intended — not to have a 'maybe' kind of sound." — Tony Williams, on what he learned from Alan Dawson

Williams said independence wasn't the rare skill — clarity was. Most drummers can play four limbs at once, but very few can play four limbs at once and have every note sound deliberate. His basic advice to drummers, said simply: "You've really got to love the instrument, and love playing no matter what music it is." (Modern Drummer 1978, JazzProfiles — Two Decades)
